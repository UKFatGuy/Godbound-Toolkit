Scadrial was a world shrouded eternally beneath a crimson sky. Each day, the meagre sunlight struggled to pierce the thick, ever-present haze of ash that drifted down ceaselessly, blanketing the ground and choking out any glimmer of hope. The relentless ashfall stained every surface, painting the landscape with shades of despair and slow decay. The land itself bore scars both ancient and fresh, a testament to generations spent locked in a battle for mere survival. Its people, marked by hardship and endurance, were worn down by the harsh realities of their environment—yet this harsh, unforgiving world was home. 
Nyx’s life among the nobility was a performance—each day consisted of a battle to maintain power, and every night a lethal battle for survival. Moving like an apparition, her presence felt more keenly in the silence between chaos than in the clamour of open conflict. She was trained to be deadly, her skills honed by necessity and the demands of House Lekal, a proud lineage that had defined itself through quiet strength and cunning manipulation.
When the world began to crumble and her family fell into ruin and eventual dissolution. She persisted, fighting for a dead family which had long since faded into dust. She navigated the ruined world with an unsettling grace, her determination outlasting even the power of pewter but even with her Allomantic strength waning, her will remained unbroken. 
In those dark times even as a Mistborn, Nyx was never the celebrated hero of legend, she had become the physical manifestation of Ruin driven almost entirely by bitterness and loss. 
As the collapse of Scadrial accelerated, Nyx grew in power and the distinction between preservation and ruin grew sharper she finally realised what she had become and despised it.
Nyx believes it was that realisation which cracked the World. It did not simply fade—it shattered with a violent suddenness, the landscape torn asunder and reality collapsing into itself. A sudden catastrophic unmaking of all that was, everything gone in but moments with Nyx at the epicentre. Simple logic dictated she should have perished alongside her world, yet something within her broke, a seeming second Mistborn ‘snap’ which brought with it more than a simple awakening of dormant power, it was a transformation beyond mortality.
What emerged was a Goddess with a bitchin’ headache.
No longer was she just a survivor or a tool of noble ambition. She had become something other—a god. With the enormity of her new existence now weighing her down, and each thought wrestling with the quiet demands of preservation and the ever-present temptation of ruin. She wandered the ruins of dead worlds, none of them her home, but some ‘other’ much stranger places with just as much wonder and variety as how own.
It was that realisation that saved her psyche from morose oblivion and eternal resignation, while her home was gone others were still at risk and she now had both the means and obligation to ‘Do Better’. While no world can never be truly saved, they need not meet such pathetic, miserable and lingering ends. So, with only a feeble recollection of the moments after she had snapped. And a soul now bearing the weight of murdered worlds, impossibly heavy, its gravity tearing at the very fabric of the universe around her she set out on her journey of ascension. Yet beneath this immense weight, there lingered a sense of sorrowful dissonance, a disquiet—a wrongness that echoed the loss of her home.
Unknown to her the destruction she had wrought was not merely physical; reality had collapsed and the metaphysical laws governing Scadrial had been rewritten its reality mauled and rent beyond recognition. Moments before the end, preservation had whispered to her fading consciousness and she had acted. Her final act was to invoke a paradox. One born of Self-loathing and unimaginable guilt & loss. She envisioned a better world, one in which she had never existed…one which with her absence had survived.

Concept & Tone
Preservation is not mercy. Preservation is, at its core, a force defined by its unwavering refusal to allow change. Unlike mercy, which is often associated with compassion, forgiveness, and the softening of suffering, Preservation stands as a cold, unyielding sentinel against the natural tides of decay and transformation. Its purpose is not to heal out of kindness but to impose stasis—to halt the progression of events, regardless of whether this is truly beneficial or just. In many ways, Preservation is the act of denying the universe its freedom to evolve or unravel, holding everything rigidly in place, for better or worse. 
Where other powers heal by restoring, Preservation heals by denying entropy. It does not mend wounds by returning them to their former state, but by refusing to admit the possibility of further damage. Its protection is not intervention in the traditional sense—it does not shield or defend out of empathy—but rather locking reality in place, ensuring nothing changes. This approach is absolute and impartial, creating a reality where suffering cannot worsen but neither can healing truly progress. 
Preservation governs:
* Stasis, binding, sealing, and fixation: Preservation is the power to freeze moments, to bind objects and people to a single, unchanging state. It is the force behind unbreakable bonds and the eternal stillness of things preserved against time.
* Restoration by enforcing an earlier state: Rather than truly healing, Preservation reverts things to how they were, ignoring all else. It does not forgive mistakes or erase pain—it simply makes it as though change never happened.
* The prevention of decay, death, and dissolution: Preservation stands guard against the inevitable decline all things face, denying the universe the opportunity to unravel or fade away.
* The containment of things that should not endure: Sometimes, Preservation imprisons suffering, malevolence, or corruption that ought to pass away, prolonging their existence beyond their natural end. This can be a blessing or a curse, depending on what is being preserved.
Used gently, it saves lives by holding back death and decay, creating tombs that breathe—places or people suspended between life and death, between change and stasis. Yet, its cold impartiality means it does not distinguish between what ought to endure and what should pass on; it simply enforces its will, regardless of consequence. 
Nyx’s Preservation is cold. It does not care whether something should continue—only whether it will be allowed to end. There is no empathy or warmth in this power, only the stark imposition of order on chaos, the freezing of moments against the ravages of time and entropy. The result is a universe where nothing changes unless Nyx allows it, and this control can be both a gift and a burden, trapping all within the confines of her will.

Concept & Tone
Ruin is not violence. Ruin is not about aggression or hostility; it does not seek to inflict damage for its own sake, nor does it revel in destruction. Instead, it is the quiet certainty that everything, in time, must yield—be it stone, steel, or spirit. The process is unhurried and inevitable, unfolding not with brutal force but with the steady hand of time. Ruin is the natural decline written into the fabric of existence, the principle behind the slow wearing away of mountains and the gentle fading of memories. It is the patient force that ensures all things fail, regardless of their strength, purpose, or will to endure. 
Where Preservation steadfastly resists change and clings to structure, Ruin insists upon transformation. Ruin is the agent of truth, revealing what lies behind the façade maintained by Preservation. It exposes the cracks, the weaknesses, and the limits that have always existed but were hidden from view. Through Ruin, the world is reminded that nothing is eternal; even the most formidable powers must one day accept change. Ruin is not a force of malicious intent but of honesty—it quietly insists on truth and transparency, refusing to allow illusion or denial to persist unchallenged. 
Ruin governs:
* Entropy, decay, corrosion, and exhaustion—these are not sudden or violent occurrences, but gradual processes that shape the destiny of all things. 
* Structural failure and systemic collapse—Ruin allows what cannot be sustained to fall away, making space for renewal and truth. 
* Betrayal, rot, and the erosion of meaning—these are the subtle signs that what was once solid now crumbles, that loyalty and purpose can be undone by time’s touch. 
* The exposure of weaknesses that were always there—Ruin does not create flaws; it unveils them, showing the truth that was always present but concealed beneath layers of denial and desire. 
Ruin does not rage. It is not a tempest or a destructive fury. Instead, it waits. Ruin is marked by patience, never hasty nor impulsive. It is the slow and steady march towards inevitable change, the quiet unraveling that persists regardless of resistance. Ruin knows that, given time, even the strongest walls must crumble, and the deepest secrets must surface. 
For Nyx, Ruin is not an act of cruelty; it is clarity. The role of Ruin is not to mete out punishment but to illuminate the truth of things. Nyx’s gift does not ask whether something should break, only identifies where it is already vulnerable. In this way, Ruin is a force for understanding and revelation, stripping away all pretence to expose what is real. Ruin is a fundamental part of the world, a counterpart to Preservation, and together they shape the cycle of existence: order maintained through constraint, and transformation realised through inevitable decline.

Concept & Tone
Singularity is not gravity; it does not pull, but rather erases the illusion that things are separate. Where gravity draws together, Singularity insists separation is a lie. In contrast to Ruin, which exposes how things fall apart, Singularity answers a deeper question—what happens when there is no more room for difference?
Singularity governs convergence, compression, and forced unity. It is the collapse of space, meaning, and identity—the threshold beyond which return is impossible. Its terror lies in the certainty that there is no outside, no distance, no escape. Once the process begins, differences vanish and all things become one.
But Singularity does not destroy; it simplifies. Complexity, nuance, and multiplicity are carved away, leaving only the singular, undivided essence. This is an intimate, quiet, and unforgivingly final transformation. There is no explosion or violence, only a relentless narrowing: options dwindle, identities blur, and all divergence is erased.
Nyx’s Singularity embodies this principle. Her presence is not aggressive but encompassing—a hush that settles with inescapable finality. Those who encounter her may sense the exact moment when all alternatives vanish and the outcome is set. There is no safe distance; escape becomes impossible as boundaries lose their meaning and space itself grows thin.
The implications reach from the personal—where two minds blur into one, erasing conflict—to the mythic, where reality’s many forms collapse into a single truth. Singularity is not chaos or destruction; it is the quiet, inevitable convergence of all things, the end of difference, and the realization that, in the end, there is only one.

Concept & Tone
Nyx’s life among the Mistborn nobility was by far the most defining part of her life it dictated her skills, training and her entire outlook on life. It should not be any surprise that it also played a major role in her divine ascension. She was trained to be deadly, her skills honed by necessity and the demands of her family.
Allomancy is not wizardry. It does not summon powers from the ether, nor twist reality at whim. Rather, Allomancy is an interaction—a meticulous art through which Nyx shapes the world in ways that feel intimate and instinctive to her. This disciplined engagement ensures she steers clear of the perils inherent in wielding raw, untamed divine force. Abandoning this measured approach, her gifts lapse into bluntness: cumbersome and distressingly direct, bereft of elegance or subtlety.   
Nyx and the Act of Allomancy

Core Qualities of Allomancy
Allomancy is realised in:
* Precision, the exactness of intention; motion, the choreography of force; leverage, the art of balance and fulcrum.
* Metal as grammar—the structure through which meaning is made manifest, not mere consumption.
* Violence translated into vectors and anchors, a physics of intent rather than impulse.
* The body as interface, the flesh and thought negotiating with forces that would otherwise remain untouched.
Nyx does not cast miracles. She acts, and the world shudders along lines it had long hidden, answering her touch. The practice is not transgression—it is the uncovering of latent paths, the invocation of possibilities long buried within the weave of existence. 
Where Allomancy meets Reality
Where ShardWords inscribe the perimeter of what may be, Allomancy is the art of reaching through the margin—Nyx’s hand upon the boundary, her will shaping the contours of potential without trespass. Allomancy is not the rewriting of law, but the recognition of the possible.

Hemalurgy in canon (Cosmere):
* It transfers attributes/powers by stealing and splicing spiritweb fragments into a metal spike, then embedding that spike into the recipient.
* The spike’s placement must involve contact with moving blood at the right bind points, and it can even be charged by a thrown spike/dart if the intent and placement are right.

* Side effect: spikes tear the spiritweb, creating “holes” that allow powerful outside influences to whisper to or control the spiked subject.
* It is one of the Metallic Arts tied to the Shards of Ruin/Preservation, and the FAQ frames the three Metallic Arts as manifestations of those Shards’ power in mortal form.

* Godbound distinguishes between low magic (schools/traditions) and theurgy (high magic) as separate kinds of power.
* This writeup makes Low Magic Hemalurgy do temporary stapling and “failurefinding” tricks, while Theurgy is where you put the permanent soulsurgery that can change campaigns.

Whenever a Scarlet Staple working creates a Charged Spike, it has a Potency rating: Faint / Strong / Keen / Perfect.
* Decay: At each sunrise, a spike outside a body drops one step of Potency (Perfect?Keen?Strong?Faint?spent). A spent spike becomes inert. (This is your table abstraction of hemalurgic decay.)
* BloodKeeping: If the spike is coated in blood and sealed in a vial, wrapped in bloody cloth, or stored in a fresh cut of meat, it does not decay at sunrise.
* Never truly zero: If you want to mirror “never completely loses its charge,” treat “spent” as “too weak for spells, but still spiritually tainted.”
GM note: The above gives you a clean knob: Hemalurgy is powerful, but it has logistics and urgency.
Apprentice Practices (minor, repeatable rituals)
These are the “always useful” tricks that make the school feel like Hemalurgy even at low levels.
1. Sight the Bind
You can identify likely bind points on a humanoid creature (or the “wrong” places that would kill/ruin the working). This doesn’t grant perfect anatomical knowledge, but it gives advantage/permission to attempt Hemalurgic rites safely.
2. BloodMark the Needle
You can coat and prepare spikes so they qualify for BloodKeeping and resist mundane corrosion, letting you keep charges stable between scenes.
3. Ruin’s Appraisal
By watching a person, object, plan, or group for a minute, you can name the most likely failure mode (“the hinge that will snap,” “the lie holding it up,” “the beam carrying the load”). This doesn’t cause the failure; it just reveals the crack—very Ruin.

Adept workings create your first “real” hemalurgic play: temporary theft and stapling.
1. Borrowed Talent (Faint Spike)

Why it fits: Canon explicitly frames Hemalurgy as attribute transfer via spiritweb theft/splicing.

3. The Cracking Word

Why it fits: Ruin is systemic collapse and inevitability, not flashy violence.

2. Stolen Investiture (Perfect Spike)
You can steal and staple a nonnative kind of investiture/innate magic in a limited way, for a day, if the target possesses it. (This is the “Hemalurgy can transfer almost any type of Investiture” idea, constrained to low-magic duration.)
Part II — Theurgy: The Red Litany of Ruin (Hemalurgic Theurgy)
Who can use this?
A Godbound with the Word of Sorcery (or a GM-approved theurgic tradition). Godbound treats theurgy as terrible High Magic that can reshape the world; this tradition should feel like that.
What Theurgy unlocks
Permanent hemalurgic changes: stable spikes, true grafts, constructs, and lasting “holes.” You’re now playing with the same moral dynamite as canon Hemalurgy: soul theft and stapling that leaves people vulnerable to outside influence.
Theurgic Law: Three Prices
Every Hemalurgic Invocation must pay at least one of these (GM’s choice, player’s choice, or table rule):
1. Blood price (harm, sacrifice, or permanent scar)
2. Identity price (memory loss, personality drift, unwanted Connection)
3. Control price (the recipient becomes easier to influence; “holes” widen)
This anchors Hemalurgy as Ruinaligned: power gained by breaking wholeness.
Invocation 1: Charge the Spike
Rite: You drive a metal spike through the correct bind point to rip and capture a fragment of spiritweb, charging the spike. Blood contact and correct placement matter.
Effect: Create one Charged Spike containing:
* a stolen attribute (strength, senses, mental fortitude, etc.), or
* a stolen supernatural “module” (a specific learned magic talent), or
* a stolen investiture fragment (GM adjudication).
Consequences: The donor is damaged; death is common (and the rite assumes it), though nonlethal charging is possible at high mastery with severe spiritual cost.
Invocation 2: Staple the Gift
Rite: Embed the charged spike into a recipient at the correct bind point, stapling the stolen fragment into their spiritweb.
Effect: The recipient gains the stolen attribute/power permanently (or until the spike is removed or destroyed).
Shadow: Each implanted spike creates holes that make the subject more vulnerable to being whispered to or controlled by sufficiently potent outside forces.
Godbound conversion suggestion:
* A single spike can grant a Fact-equivalent or a narrow “lesser gift-like edge.”
* Multiple spikes can approximate broader packages, but with escalating risks and the “control price.”
Invocation 3: Law of Hemalurgic Decay
Truth: A charged spike loses potency outside the body; most loss happens early; blood can reduce it.
Effect (gameable):
* If a charged spike is removed and left unpreserved, it loses “steps” of effectiveness quickly.
* If kept bloodcoated or “bodyfooled,” it remains potent.
* Even degraded spikes may never become fully inert, but their remaining charge becomes more like taint than utility.
This is the perfect builtin limiter for the GM: hemalurgy wants urgency.
Invocation 4: Claim the Spiked
Rite: You exploit the “open seam” created by implanted spikes to impose pressure on the target’s mind.
Effect: You may:
* issue a compulsion,
* override a hesitation,
* or force obedience for a scene (Worthy foes resist).
Lore anchor: The Coppermind summary explicitly notes spikes create holes through which Shards or powerful emotional manipulators can whisper/control.
Invocation 5: Forge the Hemalurgic Construct
Rite: A day-long surgery/ritual that uses multiple spikes to twist a body into something that can survive what should be fatal, binding foreign spiritweb fragments into a stable chassis. (This echoes how hemalurgy can warp bodies into “almost inhuman” survival states.)
Effect: Create a loyal (or dangerously unstable) Construct: enhanced strength, altered senses, unnatural resilience, etc.
Cost: Always pay Control price: constructs are easier to command, but also easier to hijack if someone else can reach the seam.
Invocation 6: False Connection
Rite: Staple a stolen Connection/Identity fragment into the recipient, forging a relationship that no one intended: allegiance, language affinity, access to local magic keyed to identity/connection, etc.
Lore anchor: Coppermind explicitly frames Hemalurgy as able to warp souls and create “false Connections” in cosmere-wide implications (quoted on the page).
These are “Godbound-style” dials: simple, decisive, and narrative-forward.
1. Spike Slots (soft cap): Past a small number of implanted spikes, the subject becomes unstable, monstrous, or NPClevel vulnerable to compulsion. (Coppermind notes there’s a maximum number before mental/physical issues; we’re adapting that.)
2. No free lunch: Every permanent gain comes with Identity drift or Control vulnerability—because that’s literally what hemalurgy does to the spiritweb.
The Scarlet Staple is not necromancy and not murdermagic. It is the craft of unmaking wholeness, of stealing the hidden loadbearing pieces of a soul and fastening them elsewhere with cold metal and moving blood. Its miracles are never loud. They feel like the moment everyone realizes the bridge was always going to fall.



The Quiet Metals are a physiological and spiritual discipline, not a spell tradition. Practitioners do not project thoughts, rewrite memories, or seize control. Instead, they manipulate emotional pressure, attentional focus, and supernatural perception by briefly altering the internal balance of a subject’s passions and awareness.
In Godbound terms, this is low magic because:
* It cannot create emotions from nothing
* It cannot force decisions
* It fails outright against divine authority unless the foe allows it
* Its strongest effects are temporary, contextual, and fragile
This is sorcery that exposes fault lines rather than imposing will.
School Drawback — The Resonant Mind
While you know any spell from this school, your own emotions are slightly louder to the unseen.
* You suffer disadvantage on saves against divine emotional influence or temptation.
* Gods, spirits, and ancient intelligences find your presence “noticeable,” like a vibration in still water.
* This does not make you easier to control—only easier to sense.
(If you are already a Godbound, treat this as narrative pressure rather than a mechanical penalty.)

The Quiet Metals do not dominate the mind. They reveal how fragile it already was. Fear, anger, loyalty, and resolve are not created—only unbalanced. The art is subtle, invasive, and deeply unsettling, for it leaves the victim knowing the thoughts were their own. – Nyx Lekal

The Kinetic Metals are a discipline of force and physiology. Practitioners do not conjure energy, summon spirits, or rewrite matter.
